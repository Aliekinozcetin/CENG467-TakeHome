\section{Question 1 -- Representation Learning in Text Classification}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Dataset and Properties}

The IMDb movie review dataset was used for binary sentiment classification (positive/negative). The full labeled pool (50{,}000 examples) was shuffled with a fixed seed (42) and split into 45{,}000 training, 2{,}500 validation, and 2{,}500 test examples. The test set was held out and used only once for final evaluation.

\subsection{Preprocessing Pipeline}

A shared preprocessing pipeline was applied to all non-transformer models: (1) lowercasing, (2) HTML tag removal (\texttt{<br />} etc.), (3) punctuation and special character stripping, and (4) English stopword removal (NLTK). DistilBERT received raw, uncleaned text to leverage its own WordPiece tokenizer.

Stopword removal reduced token count by approximately 49\%, shrinking the vocabulary from $\sim$366K to $\sim$95K unique tokens on the training set.

\subsection{Tokenization Strategy Comparison}

Two tokenization strategies were compared on the same sample text:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Strategy A -- Word-level:} Simple whitespace split after cleaning. Produces exact surface forms; unknown words become out-of-vocabulary (OOV). Sample: 34 tokens.
    \item \textbf{Strategy B -- WordPiece (DistilBERT):} Subword tokenization; rare words are decomposed into known subword units (e.g., \textit{slasher} $\to$ \textit{slash + \#\#er}), eliminating OOV entirely. Sample: 37 tokens.
\end{itemize}

WordPiece produces slightly more tokens per text due to subword splitting but provides better coverage of morphological variants and rare domain-specific terms (film titles, actor names).

Strategy A is employed by the TF-IDF and BiLSTM models, while Strategy B is used exclusively by DistilBERT. This means the two strategies are compared indirectly through model performance: differences in test accuracy between word-level and WordPiece models reflect both tokenization choice and model capacity. A key practical consequence of Strategy B is truncation: with \texttt{max\_length=192}, reviews longer than 192 subword tokens are silently cut off. Given that the average raw review is 231 tokens, a non-trivial portion of reviews lose their final sentences under Strategy B.

\subsection{Model Implementations}

Four models were trained under comparable conditions (same splits, seed=42):

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{TF-IDF + Logistic Regression:} \texttt{TfidfVectorizer} with 50{,}000 features and (1,2)-ngrams, followed by \texttt{LogisticRegression(C=1.0, max\_iter=1000)}.
    \item \textbf{TF-IDF + LinearSVC:} Same TF-IDF representation with \texttt{LinearSVC(C=1.0)}.
    \item \textbf{BiLSTM + GloVe:} 2-layer bidirectional LSTM (hidden=256), GloVe 6B 100d pretrained embeddings (fine-tuned), dropout=0.3. Trained with Adam (lr=1e-3, batch=64). Early stopping with patience=3 halted training at epoch 4.
    \item \textbf{DistilBERT-base-uncased:} Full fine-tuning of \texttt{distilbert-base-uncased} (66M parameters). AdamW (lr=3e-5), batch=8, max\_length=192, 3 epochs, linear warmup (6\% of steps), best-checkpoint saving.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Discussion}

\textbf{Sparse representations (TF-IDF)} capture discriminative n-gram patterns efficiently. With bigram features, they implicitly encode local sentiment phrases (e.g., ``not good'', ``highly recommended'') without requiring sequential modeling. Their main limitation is the bag-of-words assumption: word order and long-range dependencies are ignored entirely.

\textbf{Dense sequential representations (BiLSTM + GloVe)} model word order but face two constraints on IMDb: (1) the domain mismatch between general-purpose GloVe embeddings and film-review vocabulary, and (2) the last-hidden-state bottleneck that compresses the entire review into a fixed vector. Early stopping at epoch 4 (val\_acc peak 0.9052) prevented further overfitting, yet the model still trails TF-IDF -- a known result on IMDb where local n-gram patterns are highly predictive.

\textbf{Contextual representations (DistilBERT)} achieve the best performance by producing token embeddings sensitive to surrounding context. The pretrained model brings broad language understanding transferable to sentiment tasks. The narrow gap over TF-IDF (+0.48\% accuracy) reflects (a) max\_length=192 truncation cutting off the tail of reviews longer than 192 subword tokens -- sentiment-bearing conclusions are often lost -- and (b) the inherent discriminability of surface n-gram patterns in IMDb, which leaves little room for contextual modeling to add value. In terms of \textit{interpretability}, TF-IDF offers direct inspection of the most influential n-grams, while DistilBERT requires attention analysis or probing to understand its decisions.

\section{Question 2 -- Named Entity Recognition}

\subsection{Dataset and BIO Tagging}

The CoNLL-2003 English dataset was used for named entity recognition, containing 14{,}041 training, 3{,}250 validation, and 3{,}453 test sentences annotated with four entity types: PER (person), ORG (organisation), LOC (location), and MISC (miscellaneous). The BIO tagging scheme is used: B- marks the beginning of an entity span, I- marks continuation, and O marks non-entity tokens.

The dataset is heavily imbalanced toward the O tag, which constitutes the majority of tokens. Among entity types, MISC has the smallest support, making it the hardest class to learn.

Token-label alignment for the transformer model follows the standard strategy: the first subword token of each word receives the real NER label, while subsequent subword tokens and special tokens (\texttt{[CLS]}, \texttt{[SEP]}) are assigned \texttt{-100} and ignored in the loss computation.

\subsection{Model Implementations}

Two models were implemented and trained under consistent conditions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{BiLSTM-CRF:} Character-level CNN (char embed=30, out=50) combined with GloVe 6B 100d word embeddings (fine-tuned). 2-layer bidirectional LSTM (hidden=256), followed by a CRF decoding layer. Adam optimiser (lr=1e-3), batch=32, up to 15 epochs with early stopping (patience=3).

    \item \textbf{DistilBERT-base-cased:} Full fine-tuning of \texttt{distilbert-base-cased} for token classification (66M parameters). AdamW (lr=3e-5), batch=16, max\_length=128, 5 epochs, linear warmup (10\% of steps), best-checkpoint saving by validation F1.
\end{enumerate}

Two error categories were analysed:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Boundary errors} (B-/I- tag confusion): Both models make exactly 49 boundary errors. Common cases include multi-token entities such as ``Real Madrid'' where the model predicts I-ORG for ``Real'' instead of B-ORG, or vice versa.     \item \textbf{Type confusion errors} (entity mislabelled as wrong type): BiLSTM-CRF makes 696 type confusion errors vs.\ 429 for DistilBERT -- a 38\% reduction. The most frequent pattern in BiLSTM-CRF is PER$\to$ORG confusion (e.g., athlete names such as ``Inzamamul'', ``Germon'', ``Astle'' predicted as ORG), suggesting the model relies heavily on surface form rather than context. DistilBERT's contextual embeddings resolve most of these cases by attending to surrounding tokens (``scored'', ``played'', ``said'').
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Discussion: Role of Contextual Embeddings}

The results demonstrate a clear advantage of contextual representations over static embeddings in NER. BiLSTM-CRF relies on GloVe vectors that assign a single fixed representation to each word regardless of context -- ``Washington'' has the same embedding whether it appears as a person or a location. DistilBERT produces token embeddings conditioned on the full sentence, enabling disambiguation of polysemous named entities.

The 38\% reduction in type confusion errors directly quantifies this advantage. Boundary errors, however, are equally common in both models, indicating that span detection is driven more by sequence-level positional patterns (which CRF handles well) than by semantic understanding. This suggests that a hybrid approach -- DistilBERT embeddings with CRF decoding -- would likely push performance further.

\section{Question 3 -- Text Summarization}

\subsection{Dataset}

The CNN/DailyMail dataset (version 3.0.0) was used for evaluation. A randomly sampled subset of 1{,}000 test examples was used; no training was performed since both models operate without task-specific training on this dataset. Each example contains an \texttt{article} field (average 1{,}395 words) and a \texttt{highlights} field serving as the reference summary (average 29 words). The large article-to-summary compression ratio makes this a challenging abstractive summarisation benchmark.

\subsection{Model Implementations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{LexRank (Extractive):} Graph-based extractive summarisation using sentence similarity (TF-IDF cosine). Three sentences are selected per article via the \texttt{sumy} library. No training required; runtime $\approx$16 seconds for 1{,}000 articles.

    \item \textbf{BART-large-cnn (Abstractive):} \texttt{facebook/bart-large-cnn}, a pretrained sequence-to-sequence transformer fine-tuned on CNN/DailyMail. Direct inference via HuggingFace \texttt{pipeline}, batch size=8, MPS device. Generation parameters: max\_length=128, min\_length=30, no\_repeat\_ngram\_size=3, beam search.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Discussion: Extractive vs Abstractive Trade-offs}

\textbf{Computational cost:} LexRank requires no GPU and runs in $\sim$16 seconds for 1{,}000 articles. BART requires MPS/GPU inference and takes significantly longer, making extractive methods preferable in resource-constrained settings.

\textbf{Faithfulness:} LexRank guarantees factual faithfulness by copying verbatim sentences. BART occasionally introduces minor hallucinations or reframes facts, though no major factual errors were observed in the qualitative examples.

\textbf{Readability:} BART produces grammatically smooth, coherent summaries that read naturally. LexRank summaries can be disjointed when extracted sentences lack transitional context.

\textbf{Metric behaviour:} ROUGE and BLEU favour surface overlap and penalise valid paraphrases. BERTScore better captures semantic equivalence, as shown in Example 1 where BART's lower ROUGE co-exists with a high BERTScore. For abstractive systems, BERTScore is therefore the most informative single metric.

\section{Question 4 -- Machine Translation}

\subsection{Dataset and Preprocessing}

The Multi30k English$\to$German dataset was used: 29{,}000 training, 1{,}014 validation, and 1{,}000 test sentence pairs. Preprocessing steps: (1) spaCy tokenisation (\texttt{en\_core\_web\_sm} / \texttt{de\_core\_news\_sm}), (2) lowercasing, (3) word-level vocabulary with minimum frequency threshold of 2 (EN $\approx$5K, DE $\approx$7K tokens). The test OOV rate is 1.71\%, indicating low but non-trivial out-of-vocabulary coverage.

\subsection{Model Implementations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Seq2Seq + Bahdanau Attention (from scratch):} 1-layer BiLSTM encoder (hidden=256, embed=256), 1-layer LSTM decoder with Bahdanau attention, dropout=0.3, teacher forcing ratio=0.5. Adam (lr=1e-3), gradient clipping=1.0, batch=256, 10 epochs. Trained on CPU: Apple M4's MPS backend does not accelerate \texttt{pack\_padded\_sequence} operations, making CPU faster than MPS for recurrent models on this hardware. Greedy decoding at inference.

    \item \textbf{Helsinki-NLP/opus-mt-en-de (pretrained):} MarianMT transformer pretrained on OPUS parallel corpora. Direct inference via HuggingFace, beam search (num\_beams=5), max\_length=128.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Discussion: Metric Interpretation and Architecture Trade-offs}

\textbf{Metric interpretation:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{BLEU} computes the geometric mean of $n$-gram precisions ($n=1,\ldots,4$) with a brevity penalty. Because it uses a geometric mean, a near-zero precision at any order collapses the entire score. In Seq2Seq output, repetitive tokens (``etwas etwas etwas'', ``einen einen einen'') produce valid 1-gram matches but destroy 2-gram and higher precision, driving the geometric mean toward zero. This explains BLEU=3.51 despite ChrF=38.99: character-level overlap survives repetition, but 4-gram surface precision does not.
    \item \textbf{ChrF} operates at the character n-gram level and is robust to German morphological inflection (e.g., ``orangefarbener'' vs ``orangefarbene''). Seq2Seq's ChrF=38.99 shows it has learned German word forms even when full phrases are wrong.
    \item \textbf{METEOR} aligns hypotheses to references with stemming and synonym matching, making it more recall-oriented. Seq2Seq's METEOR=0.457 confirms partial semantic coverage.
    \item \textbf{BERTScore} uses contextual embeddings to measure semantic similarity. The gap (0.7415 vs 0.8972) reflects the overall quality difference; Helsinki's translations are semantically closer to references at the representation level.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Architecture trade-offs:} Seq2Seq trained from scratch on 29K sentence pairs in 10 epochs cannot compete with a pretrained transformer exposed to millions of parallel sentences. However, the from-scratch model demonstrates that attention-based Seq2Seq can learn basic German morphology and sentence structure (ChrF$\approx$39, METEOR$\approx$0.46) under limited data and compute. Beam search and subword tokenisation would be the two highest-impact improvements for the Seq2Seq model.

\section{Question 5 -- Language Modeling}

\subsection{Dataset and Vocabulary}

The WikiText-2 dataset was used for word-level language modeling. The corpus contains 2{,}075{,}677 training tokens, 216{,}347 validation tokens, and 244{,}102 test tokens. A shared vocabulary was built from the training set using a minimum frequency threshold of 2, yielding approximately 33{,}K unique tokens. Rare words are mapped to \texttt{<unk>}; each sentence is terminated with an \texttt{<eos>} token. All text is lowercased and whitespace-tokenised.

\subsection{Model Implementations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Trigram LM (Kneser-Ney smoothing):} An interpolated Kneser-Ney trigram model with fixed discount $d=0.75$. The model uses continuation probability for the unigram backoff:
    \[
      P_{\mathrm{KN}}(w_3 \mid w_1, w_2) = \frac{\max(c(w_1,w_2,w_3)-d,\,0)}{c(w_1,w_2)} + \lambda(w_1,w_2)\,P_{\mathrm{KN}}(w_3 \mid w_2)
    \]
    No training beyond counting; perplexity computed on the full test set.

    \item \textbf{LSTM LM (2-layer, weight tying):} A 2-layer LSTM with embedding dimension=512, hidden size=512, dropout=0.5. Weight tying is applied between the input embedding matrix and the output projection layer, reducing parameter count and improving generalisation. Trained with AdamW (lr=$10^{-3}$, weight\_decay=$10^{-5}$), gradient clipping=1.0, batch=32, BPTT=35, 20 epochs. Learning rate is halved by \texttt{ReduceLROnPlateau} (patience=2) when validation perplexity stops improving. Best checkpoint is saved by validation perplexity.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Discussion: Statistical vs Neural Language Models}

\textbf{Smoothing and sparsity:} The trigram model with Kneser-Ney smoothing handles unseen n-grams by backing off to lower-order distributions weighted by continuation probability. This is effective for short contexts but fundamentally limited to a 3-token window. PPL=288.87 on WikiText-2 is consistent with the literature for KN trigrams on this benchmark.

\textbf{Long-range dependencies:} The LSTM hidden state theoretically captures unbounded context, which explains its 23\% PPL reduction over the trigram (219 vs 288). The improvement is moderate rather than dramatic because (a) 20 epochs is insufficient for full convergence -- the training curve shows no plateau -- and (b) word-level tokenisation with \texttt{<unk>} replacement limits coverage of rare WikiText-2 tokens (names, technical terms).

\textbf{Optimiser choice:} Switching from SGD (LR=10, clip=0.5) to AdamW (LR=$10^{-3}$, clip=1.0) was critical. With SGD, 30 epochs produced val PPL=364 and the LR decay triggered only once (epoch 19). AdamW's adaptive per-parameter learning rates produced val PPL=229 in 20 epochs, a 37\% improvement, confirming that adaptive optimisers are better suited to the noisy gradient landscape of word-level language modeling.

\textbf{Weight tying:} Sharing the input embedding and output projection matrices reduces the model's effective parameter count and acts as a regulariser, preventing the output layer from memorising frequency statistics independently of the learned representations.

\section{Conclusion}
% ============================================================

This report implemented and evaluated two models per task across five core NLP problems. The key findings are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Text Classification (Q1):} DistilBERT achieves the highest test accuracy (91.8\%), but the margin over TF-IDF+SVM (+0.48\%) is narrow, confirming that surface n-gram patterns are highly discriminative on IMDb. Error analysis reveals that negation and irony are the dominant failure modes for all models.

    \item \textbf{Named Entity Recognition (Q2):} DistilBERT-base-cased outperforms BiLSTM-CRF by +4.71 F1 on CoNLL-2003. The 38\% reduction in type confusion errors directly quantifies the benefit of contextual embeddings for disambiguating polysemous entity names.

    \item \textbf{Text Summarization (Q3):} BART-large-cnn outperforms LexRank across all seven metrics. BERTScore is the most informative metric for abstractive systems, as it captures semantic equivalence that ROUGE and BLEU penalise as paraphrase.

    \item \textbf{Machine Translation (Q4):} Helsinki-NLP/opus-mt-en-de (BLEU=36.37) substantially outperforms the from-scratch Seq2Seq model (BLEU=3.51). The low BLEU of Seq2Seq is explained by token repetition in greedy decoding collapsing the geometric mean of n-gram precisions, not by a total failure of learning -- ChrF=38.99 and METEOR=0.457 confirm partial morphological and semantic coverage.

    \item \textbf{Language Modeling (Q5):} The 2-layer weight-tied LSTM with AdamW achieves test PPL=219.28, a 24\% reduction over the Kneser-Ney trigram baseline (288.87). The optimiser switch from SGD to AdamW alone reduced perplexity by 37\% under the same epoch budget, highlighting the importance of optimiser choice for recurrent language models.
\end{itemize}

Across all tasks, the general pattern holds: pretrained and contextual models outperform statistical and from-scratch neural baselines, but the gap varies substantially by task. On IMDb sentiment classification, the gap is negligible; on NER and translation, it is large. The common bottleneck for from-scratch neural models is training data and compute: given sufficient resources, Seq2Seq and LSTM models approach competitive performance, as the PPL and ChrF results demonstrate.
